Stage 1 · Weeks 1–4

The Beginner's Mind

Before touching Figma, build the mental model. Great designers are empathetic problem-solvers first, visual craftspeople second. This stage rewires how you see products around you.

What is Product Design?

UX Design — The architecture of experience. How does a user flow from problem to solution? Covers research, IA, flows, wireframes.

UI Design — The visual surface. Typography, color, components, spacing. The "skin" that users see and touch.

Product Design — The synthesis of both, plus product strategy, metrics awareness, and cross-functional collaboration. A Product Designer is a business partner.

Interaction Design (IxD) — The behavior layer. How does the product respond to user actions? Animations, transitions, micro-interactions.

💡 Key Insight: Top companies don't hire "just" UX or "just" UI designers anymore. They hire Product Designers who can own the full problem-to-solution journey.

HCI is the academic foundation of UX. It studies how people interact with computers and what makes those interactions effective, efficient, and satisfying.

Core Models to Learn:

  • Don Norman's Action Cycle — 7 stages from goal formation to evaluation. Understanding where users get "stuck" in this cycle reveals your design's friction points.
  • Gulf of Execution — The gap between what users want to do and what the interface allows. Reduce this gap with clear affordances.
  • Gulf of Evaluation — The gap between system state and user understanding. Reduce this with clear feedback.
  • GOMS Model — Goals, Operators, Methods, Selection rules. A framework for predicting user task completion time.
Study: Read "The Design of Everyday Things" by Don Norman. It's not optional — it's the industry bible.

Users don't read, they scan. They don't think, they react. Understanding cognition is your superpower.

  • Cognitive Load — The mental effort required to use your interface. Reduce extraneous load; increase germane (meaningful) load. Split complex tasks into steps.
  • Working Memory — Humans hold ~4 chunks of info in working memory at once. Don't make users remember across screens — show it to them.
  • Attention — Pre-attentive processing happens in <200ms. Color, size, and motion capture attention before conscious thought.
  • Mental Models — Users approach your product with expectations built from prior experience. If your UI violates their mental model, they feel confused and leave.
  • Anchoring Bias — The first piece of information seen sets expectations. Use this strategically in pricing, onboarding, and comparisons.
  • Peak-End Rule — People judge experiences by the peak moment and the ending, not the average. Design memorable peaks and satisfying endings.

UCD is a philosophy, not a methodology. It means every decision — from button color to checkout flow — starts with the user's context, goals, and constraints.

The 4 Principles:

  1. Understand the user — Research before assumptions. Who are they? What's their context? What are their mental models?
  2. Involve users early — Don't design in isolation. Test rough concepts before building polished ones.
  3. Iterative design — No design is perfect on first try. Prototype → Test → Learn → Repeat.
  4. Evaluate against user needs — Measure success by task completion and satisfaction, not aesthetics.

The Stanford d.school framework — the most widely adopted design process in the industry:

EmpathizeObserve, interview, and immerse yourself in the user's world. Never skip this. Most bad products are the result of assumed empathy.
DefineSynthesize research into a clear problem statement (How Might We...). A well-framed problem is 80% of the solution.
IdeateGenerate many ideas without judgment. Use techniques: Crazy 8s, SCAMPER, mind mapping, How Might We statements.
PrototypeBuild the cheapest possible version that tests your key assumption. Paper prototypes are valid.
TestPut prototypes in front of real users. Watch, listen, stay silent. Update your assumptions.

These are not guidelines — they're laws of usable interfaces. Memorize them. Apply them in every critique.

1. Visibility of system status
Always keep users informed about what's going on (progress bars, loading states, confirmation messages).
2. Match with real world
Use familiar language and concepts, not system terminology. Speak the user's language.
3. User control & freedom
Users make mistakes. Provide clear undo and redo. Don't trap users.
4. Consistency & standards
Follow platform conventions. Don't make users guess if different words/icons mean the same thing.
5. Error prevention
The best error message is one that never has to appear. Design to prevent mistakes.
6. Recognition over recall
Minimize memory load. Make options visible — don't make users remember from previous screens.
7. Flexibility & efficiency
Offer shortcuts for expert users. Accelerators (keyboard shortcuts, gestures) for power users.
8. Aesthetic & minimalist design
Don't add elements that compete with important information. Every extra element costs attention.
9. Help users recognize & recover
Error messages should explain the problem in plain language and suggest a solution.
10. Help & documentation
Even well-designed products may need documentation. Make it searchable and task-focused.

Accessibility is not charity — it's good design. 1 in 4 adults has a disability. WCAG defines conformance at A, AA, and AAA levels. Most products target AA.

The 4 POUR Principles:

Perceivable
Information must be presentable in ways users can perceive. Alt text for images, captions for video, sufficient color contrast (4.5:1 for normal text).
Operable
UI must be operable by all users. Keyboard navigability, no seizure-inducing animations, sufficient time to complete tasks.
Understandable
Information and UI operation must be understandable. Predictable behavior, clear error identification, input assistance.
Robust
Content must be parseable by assistive technologies. Semantic HTML, ARIA roles, landmark navigation.

Research Foundations & Seminal Studies
Why this matters: Great designers don't just follow rules — they understand why the rules exist. These are the foundational studies and theories that the entire discipline is built on.
The Psychology of Human-Computer Interaction (1983)
Card, Moran & Newell
Introduced the GOMS model and the Keystroke-Level Model (KLM) — the first systematic framework for predicting how long computer tasks take. Established HCI as a legitimate cognitive science discipline. Free summary available via ACM Digital Library.
Affordances and Design (1988) — The Psychology of Everyday Things
Donald Norman
Introduced "affordances" to design — the perceived and actual properties that determine how an object can be used. The door handle that looks pushable but must be pulled is the most famous design failure example. Norman's work is the reason UX exists as a profession.
Usability Engineering (1993)
Jakob Nielsen
The research basis for the 10 heuristics and the finding that 5 users catch 85% of usability issues. Nielsen's mathematical model for optimal testing sample size changed how the entire industry conducts research. Core reference for any UX practitioner.
Dual Process Theory (Thinking, Fast and Slow)
Daniel Kahneman (2011) — building on Tversky & Kahneman research from 1970s–80s
System 1 (fast, automatic, intuitive) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical). Most UX decisions happen in System 1. Design for System 1 defaults; don't force System 2 unnecessarily. Explains why cognitive load reduction is not optional.
Cognitive Load Theory (1988)
John Sweller
Distinguishes between intrinsic load (task complexity), extraneous load (poor design), and germane load (learning/schema formation). UX designers fight extraneous cognitive load. This theory justifies progressive disclosure, chunking, and minimalism with scientific rigor.
Activity Theory in HCI
Leontiev / Vygotsky (adapted by Nardi & others for HCI, 1990s)
Analyzes human activity as composed of goals (why), actions (what), and operations (how). When operations become automatic they become habits — explains why changing established interaction patterns is so difficult and costly for users.
ISO 9241-210: Human-Centred Design for Interactive Systems (2019)
International Organization for Standardization
The international standard that formally defines user-centered design. Specifies 6 principles: designing based on explicit understanding of users, tasks, environments; involving users throughout; iterative design; addressing the whole user experience; multidisciplinary teams. Referenced in almost every professional design contract.

Practice Exercises — Stage 1
Exercise 1 — Heuristic Violation Hunt (3 hrs)
01Choose any 3 apps: one mobile banking, one e-commerce, one food delivery.
02Screenshot 2–3 screens from each app.
03For every Nielsen heuristic, find at least one violation AND one strong example across your 9 screens.
04Create a slide or Notion doc: screenshot → heuristic violated/applied → your explanation → proposed improvement.
GOALAfter this exercise you'll never look at an interface without automatically heuristic-evaluating it.
Exercise 2 — Design Thinking Sprint (1 day)
01Pick a local problem you personally experience (commuting, grocery shopping, studying).
02Interview 2 people about their experience with the problem. Write verbatim notes.
03Write a "How Might We" statement from your notes.
04Sketch 8 ideas in 8 minutes (Crazy 8s). No judgment — quantity over quality.
05Pick 1 idea and sketch a rough user flow (paper is fine).
GOALExperience the full design thinking loop in one day. Identify where it felt uncomfortable — that's your growth edge.
Exercise 3 — Cognitive Load Audit (1 hr)
01Open any complex settings screen (iOS Settings or Google account settings).
02Identify: Which elements cause extraneous cognitive load? Which cause intrinsic load? What decisions did the designers make to reduce load?
03Redesign one section on paper to reduce cognitive load without reducing functionality.
GOALBuild the habit of reading interfaces through the lens of Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory.
Exercise 4 — Mental Model Mapping (2 hrs)
01Ask 3 non-designer friends to describe in their own words "how Spotify recommends music." Record their exact language.
02Draw the mental model each person described.
03Compare their mental models with how Spotify's recommendation engine actually works.
04Identify the "gulf of evaluation" — where reality diverges from user mental models.
GOALUnderstand how mental models are constructed and how they shape interface expectations.
Stage 2 · Months 2–3

The Intellectual Foundation

UX laws, visual fundamentals, and the grammar of design. This stage transforms you from someone who makes things "look nice" into a designer who can defend every decision with evidence.

UX Laws & Psychology — Applied

These aren't academic curiosities — every law below is directly applied by product teams at Spotify, Google, and Apple. Study the definition, then learn the application.

🎯Fitts's Law

The time to reach a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target. Larger, closer targets are faster to click.

APPLICATION → Primary CTAs should be large and near the user's natural thumb zone on mobile. Corner elements (like macOS dock) exploit screen edges as infinite-size targets. Never make important actions tiny or far from where the user's cursor naturally sits.
Real Example: Apple's iPhone home button was massive and centered — the most-used action gets prime real estate. Figma's toolbar pins the most frequent tools to keyboard shortcuts AND the most accessible screen positions.
⚡Hick's Law

Decision time increases logarithmically with the number of choices presented. More options = more friction.

APPLICATION → Never show all options simultaneously. Progressive disclosure: show 3–5 primary options, reveal advanced options behind a 'More' control. Navigation menus should be ruthlessly curated. Reduce cognitive overhead in onboarding by splitting multi-choice decisions across steps.
Real Example: Netflix's genre filter limits visible options. Spotify's 'Your Top Mixes' gives 6 playlists — not 200. iOS's app switcher shows recent apps, not all apps. Hick's Law is why great onboarding has 1 question per screen.
🔄Jakob's Law

Users spend most of their time on other sites. They prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know.

APPLICATION → Innovation should solve real problems, not reinvent conventions for novelty. Use standard patterns: hamburger menus, bottom nav bars, pull-to-refresh. When you break conventions, you add cognitive overhead. Reserve creativity for brand differentiation, not interaction patterns.
Real Example: Every e-commerce app has a cart icon top-right. Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter all have a bottom nav bar. These are conventions — breaking them requires a very compelling reason.
🔢Miller's Law

The average person can hold 7 (±2) items in their working memory at once. Modern research suggests it's closer to 4 chunks.

APPLICATION → Chunk information into groups of 4–5 maximum. Phone numbers use grouping (555-867-5309) for this reason. Navigation menus should have fewer than 7 items. Form fields should be grouped semantically. Progress steps should be 3–5 stages, not 10.
Real Example: Credit card fields split into 4-digit groups. iPhone passcode: 6 digits max. Google's search results: 10 items per page. Well-designed settings screens group related options into named sections.
✨Aesthetic–Usability Effect

Users perceive aesthetically pleasing design as more usable, even when it isn't. Beautiful design creates a halo of trust.

APPLICATION → First impressions take 50ms. Beautiful products get more forgiveness for usability issues. Invest in visual polish on onboarding and first-use screens. However, don't use this as an excuse to sacrifice usability — the effect diminishes with continued use.
Real Example: Apple products are seen as 'easier to use' partly because they're beautiful. Users rate attractive medical devices as safer and more effective. Airlines design beautiful check-in kiosks to reduce perceived wait time anxiety.
🔵Gestalt Principles

The mind perceives objects as organized patterns rather than individual elements. The whole is greater than the sum of its parts.

APPLICATION → Proximity: Elements close together are seen as related — use spacing to communicate grouping. Similarity: Similar elements (color, shape) are perceived as a group. Continuity: The eye follows implied lines. Closure: Minds complete incomplete shapes. Figure-Ground: Elements exist as subject or background.
Real Example: Cards use proximity and closure. Tab bars use similarity. Carousels crop items to signal continuation (continuity). Overlapping modals use figure-ground. Every well-designed UI uses all 5 principles simultaneously.
⚖️Tesler's Law

Every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be reduced. It can only be shifted — either to the system or to the user.

APPLICATION → Your job as a designer is to absorb complexity on behalf of the user, not simplify by hiding features they need. When simplifying, ask: 'Are we reducing complexity or shifting it?' Google's search box is simple — but the indexing complexity is massive.
Real Example: Gmail's auto-categorization absorbs the complexity of email sorting. Airbnb's price breakdown absorbs the complexity of fee calculation. iPhone's auto-brightness absorbs the complexity of display management.
📋Zeigarnik Effect

People remember uncompleted tasks better than completed ones. Unfinished business creates psychological tension that drives action.

APPLICATION → Use progress indicators, completion percentages, and 'resume' prompts to exploit this effect. Incomplete profile warnings ('Your profile is 60% complete') are more compelling than blank states. Streaks (Duolingo) leverage Zeigarnik to drive daily retention.
Real Example: LinkedIn's profile completion bar. Duolingo's daily streak counter. Airbnb's 'Continue to checkout' emails. Medium's reading progress bar at the top of articles. All leverage the Zeigarnik tension.
Visual Design Fundamentals
Color Psychology
RedUrgency, passion, danger, appetite. Used by Netflix, YouTube, Coca-Cola. Excellent for CTAs and error states.
BlueTrust, stability, calm, technology. Used by Facebook, LinkedIn, PayPal, Twitter. Dominant in enterprise and fintech.
GreenGrowth, success, nature, health. Used by Spotify, WhatsApp, Robinhood. Perfect for success states and financial apps.
Yellow/OrangeOptimism, energy, warmth, affordability. Used by Amazon, McDonald's, Bumble. Attention-grabbing — use sparingly.
PurpleLuxury, creativity, spirituality. Used by Cadbury, Hallmark. Common in beauty and creative tools.
Black/WhiteSophistication, clarity, premium. Apple uses black for hardware, white for interface. Creates focus and breathing room.
Color Harmony Systems
Monochromatic: Single hue, varying saturation/lightness. Feels sophisticated and cohesive. Great for focused, minimal interfaces.
Analogous: Adjacent hues on the wheel. Feels natural and pleasing. Common in nature-inspired or calming apps.
Complementary: Opposite hues. High contrast. Use one as dominant, other as accent. Creates visual energy.
Triadic: 3 equidistant hues. Vibrant and balanced. Complex to execute — use one dominant, two accents.
Dark Mode Design
  • Never use pure #000000 black — use dark grays (#0A0A0F, #1A1A2E) for depth
  • Elevation in dark mode is shown with lighter surface colors, not shadows
  • Reduce saturation of brand colors in dark mode — they appear harsh on dark backgrounds
  • Text hierarchy: primary #FFFFFF, secondary #AAAAAA, disabled #555555
  • Maintain 4.5:1 contrast ratio even on dark surfaces
Font Anatomy
Baseline: Invisible line that letters sit on. Determines visual alignment.
x-Height: Height of lowercase letters. High x-height = more readable at small sizes.
Serif: Small strokes at the end of letterforms. Times New Roman, Garamond. Traditional, readable in long print.
Sans-serif: Without serifs. Helvetica, SF Pro. Clean, modern, preferred for screens.
Tracking: Space between all characters. Tighter = modern luxury. Wider = titles and labels.
Leading: Space between lines. 1.5×–1.8× font size for body text. Tighter for headlines.
Font Pairing Strategy
  • Pair a display serif with a sans-serif body font for contrast (Playfair Display + Inter)
  • Use type superfamilies (IBM Plex Serif + IBM Plex Sans) for guaranteed harmony
  • Limit to 2 typefaces per interface — 3 maximum with a clear role for each
  • Never pair two fonts with similar personalities — create tension and hierarchy
Typographic Hierarchy
H1: Display/Hero — 48–96px, bold, tight tracking
H2: Section title — 28–40px, semibold
H3: Subsection — 20–24px, medium/semibold
Body: Paragraphs — 15–18px, regular, 1.6× line-height
Caption: Metadata, labels — 11–13px, regular or medium
Overline: Category labels — 11px, uppercase, tracked

12-Column Grid — Industry standard for web. Provides maximum flexibility for 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6-column layouts. Used by Material Design and Bootstrap.

8px Base Grid — All spacing values are multiples of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64...). Creates mathematical harmony across the entire interface.

Auto Layout in Figma — The modern way to implement grid thinking. Every component should use Auto Layout with consistent gap and padding values tied to your spacing system.

Spacing Psychology
  • White space (negative space) is not empty — it's a design element that creates breathing room and focus
  • Tight spacing signals density and efficiency (dashboards, data tables)
  • Generous spacing signals luxury and calm (Apple, Airbnb, luxury brands)
  • The Law of Proximity (Gestalt) means spacing communicates relationships

Robin Williams' CRAP principles — the grammar of visual design:

Contrast

Make different things look different. If two elements aren't the same, make them very different — not slightly different. Slight differences look like mistakes; strong differences look intentional.

Repetition

Repeat visual elements to create consistency and unity. Colors, typefaces, spacing, icons, shapes — repetition creates a design system, not just a screen.

Alignment

Every element should have a visual connection to something else on the page. Avoid arbitrary placement. Left-align text blocks, align icons to baselines, align CTAs to grid columns.

Proximity

Group related items together. Separate unrelated items. Proximity creates visual structure that users can parse at a glance without reading.

Research Foundations — UX Laws & Visual Design Science
The science behind the craft: Every UX law has an origin study. Every visual design principle has a scientific or perceptual basis. Knowing the why lets you apply these principles correctly — and know when to break them.
Fitts's Law: "The Information Capacity of the Human Motor System" (1954)
Paul Fitts — Journal of Experimental Psychology
Fitts derived his model from information theory, treating movement as a signal transmission problem. His original experiment used metal plates and a stylus. The mathematical model (MT = a + b log₂(2D/W)) predicts movement time with remarkable accuracy — validated in hundreds of subsequent studies and extended to touchscreens by Scott MacKenzie in 1992.
Hick's Law: "On the Rate of Gain of Information" (1952)
William Edmund Hick — Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology
Hick measured reaction time to visual stimuli and found a logarithmic relationship with the number of stimulus-response alternatives. Ray Hyman later confirmed and extended it (1953). Critical nuance designers miss: Hick's Law applies to choice reaction time, not complex decision-making — it's most powerful for navigation and option selection.
Miller's Law: "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" (1956)
George A. Miller — Psychological Review
One of the most cited papers in psychology. Miller showed that short-term memory capacity is roughly 7 ± 2 "chunks." Modern research (Cowan, 2001) suggests 4 ± 1 chunks is more accurate. Key insight: the size of a chunk is variable — experienced users chunk information more efficiently than novices, meaning expert UX differs from novice UX.
Gestalt Psychology Origins — Berlin School (1890s–1930s)
Wertheimer, Köhler, Koffka
Gestalt principles emerged from experimental psychology research on visual perception. "The whole is other than the sum of its parts" (Koffka). The principles of proximity, similarity, closure, and continuity were derived from controlled perceptual experiments. Applied directly to UI design by Robin Williams in "The Non-Designer's Design Book" (1994).
Aesthetic-Usability Effect: "Apparent Usability vs Inherent Usability" (1995)
Masaaki Kurosu & Kaori Kashimura — CHI Conference Proceedings
Japanese researchers showed two versions of ATM interfaces to participants — aesthetically pleasing vs. plain. Both had identical functionality. Users consistently rated the beautiful version as more usable, even when they weren't. Confirmed by Don Norman who noted that positive emotions improve cognitive flexibility and problem-solving ability.
Zeigarnik Effect: "On Finished and Unfinished Tasks" (1927)
Bluma Zeigarnik — Psychologische Forschung
Zeigarnik was inspired by a waiter who could remember unpaid orders perfectly but forgot them immediately after payment. Her lab studies confirmed that incomplete tasks create a persistent cognitive "tension" that improves recall. Applied in UX through progress indicators, streaks, and "continue where you left off" patterns.
Visual Design Science — Perception & Color Theory
Pre-attentive Processing in Visual Search
Anne Treisman — Feature Integration Theory (1980)
Treisman showed that certain visual features (color, size, orientation, motion) are processed in parallel, pre-consciously, in <200ms. This is why a red button "pops out" from a gray page — it doesn't require focused attention. Explains why color, size, and weight are the most powerful tools for visual hierarchy.
The Interaction of Color (1963)
Josef Albers — Yale University Press
Albers demonstrated that color perception is entirely contextual — the same color looks different depending on surrounding colors. His experiments showed that color harmony and contrast must be evaluated in context, never in isolation. His grid exercises are still the gold standard for training color perception. PDF excerpts freely available.
Universal Principles of Design — Visual Hierarchy Research
Lidwell, Holden & Butler (2003)
Synthesizes 125 design principles from psychology, architecture, and visual communication research. The visual hierarchy, signal-to-noise ratio, and chunking sections are directly applicable to UI design and cite primary research from perceptual psychology.
Eye-Tracking Research: "F-Pattern" and "Layer Cake" Reading (2006)
Jakob Nielsen / NNG — Eye Tracking Studies
NNG's eye-tracking study of 232 users found that web page reading follows an "F-pattern" — two horizontal sweeps followed by a vertical scan. Later research identified "Layer Cake" and "Spotted" patterns for different content types. Critical for understanding where users actually look on a page and how to structure information hierarchy.

Stage 3 · Months 4–6

The Process Designer

Now you execute the full UX process: research, synthesis, architecture, prototyping, and testing. This is where designers earn their seat at the product table.

UX Research Mastery
Generative Research (Discover)
Semi-structured Interviews

Open-ended conversations (60–90 min) to understand goals, pain points, and mental models. Never lead the witness — ask 'Tell me about a time when...' not 'Do you like X?'

Contextual Inquiry

Observe users in their natural environment (home, office). Reveals unspoken behaviors that interviews miss. Most valuable and most underused method.

Diary Studies

Participants self-document experiences over time (days/weeks). Captures moments the researcher can't be present for. Great for habitual behaviors.

Secondary Research

Analyze existing data: support tickets, reviews, social media, analytics, competitor analysis. Always start here — don't recreate insights that already exist.

Surveys

Quantitative data from large samples. Use for measuring attitudes, frequency, and demographics. Don't use for 'why' questions.

Evaluative Research (Validate)
Usability Testing

5 users find 85% of usability issues. Recruit realistic users. Give tasks, not directions. Note confusion, hesitation, and verbalizations. Test early and often.

A/B Testing

Compare two variants with a statistically significant user group. Quantitative validation only — use qualitative research to understand why one variant wins.

Card Sorting

Users group content into categories they create. Reveals users' mental models for information architecture. Use Optimal Workshop for remote sessions.

Tree Testing

Test the navigability of your IA structure using text-only hierarchy. Validates IA without visual design distraction.

What a persona is NOT: A demographic profile. Demographics tell you who someone is, not why they behave as they do.

What a persona IS: A behavioral archetype built from research patterns. What goals does this person have? What are their fears? What job-to-be-done are they hiring your product for?

Jobs-to-be-Done Framework

Clayton Christensen's JTBD: People don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

  • Cluster notes into themes (don't start with predetermined themes)
  • Name each cluster with an insight statement, not a category label
  • Identify the 3–5 most impactful insights that should drive design decisions

IA is the structural design of shared information environments. It determines how content is organized, labeled, and navigated.

Hierarchical: Tree structure. Most common for websites. Clear parent-child relationships.
Sequential: Linear flow. Ideal for onboarding, checkout, forms.
Matrix: Cross-referenced content. Users choose their own navigation path.
Hub & Spoke: Central hub with branches that don't connect to each other. Common in mobile apps.

Micro-interactions are the moments that make a product feel alive. Dan Saffer's framework: Trigger → Rules → Feedback → Loops & Modes.

Essential States to Design
DefaultThe resting state. Must communicate what's possible (affordance).
HoverDesktop only. Preview of what interaction will do. Subtle feedback — don't over-animate.
LoadingNever show raw spinners. Show meaningful progress. Skeleton screens > spinners for content loading.
ErrorSpecific, human-language messages. Tell the user WHAT went wrong AND HOW to fix it. Inline, not modal.
EmptyThe most neglected state. Include illustration, context, and a clear CTA. Never just "No results found."
SuccessCelebrate completion. Motion, color change, sound (if appropriate). Make the user feel accomplished.

Motion communicates. It can orient the user, communicate relationships, show continuity, and provide delight. Bad motion is worse than no motion.

Easing Reference
ease-in — Starts slow, ends fast. Use for elements exiting the screen.
ease-out — Starts fast, ends slow. Use for elements entering the screen. The most natural motion.
ease-in-out — Both. Use for elements moving within the screen.
spring — Overshoot + settle. Feels physical and alive. Used heavily by iOS and Android.

Duration Guide: Micro-interactions: 100–300ms. Screen transitions: 250–400ms. Complex animations: 400–700ms. Never exceed 1 second unless intentionally cinematic.

Research Foundations — User Research Science
Research rigor separates good UX from great UX. These foundational studies explain why certain research methods work, what their statistical limits are, and how to interpret findings without introducing bias.
"Why You Only Need to Test with 5 Users" (2000)
Jakob Nielsen — NNG Research Report
Nielsen's mathematical proof that 5 users find 85% of usability problems, based on a probability formula: p(x) = 1 - (1-L)^n where L is the probability any one user finds a problem. The diminishing returns curve shows that adding more users past 5 gives negligible new insights for the cost. Groundbreaking because it made user testing accessible to every budget.
The Jobs-to-be-Done Framework — Milkshake Study (2003)
Clayton Christensen — Harvard Business School
Christensen's famous McDonald's milkshake study: researchers found that 40% of milkshakes were purchased at 7am by lone commuters who needed a filling, slow-to-consume product to occupy their boring commute. The "job" had nothing to do with hunger or taste. Demolished demographic segmentation in product design and established JTBD as the lens for understanding true user motivation.
Contextual Design (1997)
Karen Holtzblatt & Hugh Beyer
Established contextual inquiry as a formal research method. Showed that users behave differently when observed in their natural environment vs. a lab. The "Master-Apprentice" model of research (user as master, researcher as apprentice) is still the gold standard for contextual interview technique — respectful, non-leading, and context-rich.
Measuring the User Experience (2008)
Tullis & Albert
The definitive reference on quantitative UX metrics. Covers task success, time-on-task, error rates, efficiency, and satisfaction metrics. Explains statistical significance and sample size requirements for reliable data. Every UX researcher should understand the difference between descriptive and inferential statistics in the context of usability data.
Mental Models: Aligning Design Strategy with Human Behavior (2008)
Indi Young
Young's methodology for building "mental model diagrams" — visual maps that align user tasks and goals with product features. Based on 10+ years of large-scale contextual interview research. Introduced the distinction between task-based thinking and goal-based design. Highly influential in enterprise and complex UX design.
Information Architecture: For the Web and Beyond (1998, 4th ed. 2015)
Morville, Rosenfeld & Arango ("The Polar Bear Book")
The foundational text for information architecture. Establishes the IA components (organization systems, labeling systems, navigation systems, search systems) and the research methods for designing them (card sorting, tree testing). The "three circles" model (users, content, context) is the IA designer's core mental model.

Stage 4 · Months 7–9

The Strategic Designer

Product thinking, design systems, and business impact. This is where you stop being a pixel-pusher and become a product partner. The work here separates mid-level from senior designers.

Product Thinking

Great designers don't just solve problems — they question which problems are worth solving. Product thinking means understanding market context, business constraints, and user needs simultaneously.

5 Whys: Ask 'why' 5 times to reach root cause. Surface-level problems are symptoms; root causes are solvable.
How Might We (HMW): Transform problem statements into design opportunities without prescribing solutions.
Jobs-to-be-Done: What job is the user hiring this product to do? Functional, social, and emotional dimensions.
Opportunity Solution Tree: Teresa Torres' framework. Maps desired outcome → opportunities → solutions → experiments.
Double Diamond: Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver. Equal time exploring (diverging) and focusing (converging).
North Star Metric

The single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers. Everything else is either an input metric (leading indicators) or an output metric (business outcomes).

Examples: Spotify → Daily Listening Hours | Airbnb → Nights Booked | LinkedIn → Monthly Active Users Who Apply to Jobs

AARRR Pirate Metrics
Acquisition How do users find and sign up? Measure: sign-up rate, channel attribution.
Activation Do users have their 'aha moment'? Measure: onboarding completion, feature adoption day 1.
Retention Do users come back? The most important metric. Measure: D1/D7/D30 retention.
Referral Do users recommend? Measure: NPS, invite rate, viral coefficient (K-factor).
Revenue Do users pay? Measure: ARPU, LTV, conversion rate, MRR.
Design Systems Architecture

A design system is a shared language between designers and developers. It's not a UI kit — it's a living product that enables consistent, scalable design across teams.

Foundations / Tokens

The most primitive decisions: color palette, type scale, spacing scale, border radii, shadow scale, motion curves. These become design tokens (variables) that propagate everywhere.

Components

Reusable UI building blocks: Button, Input, Card, Modal, Toast, Badge, Avatar, Navigation. Each with all variants and states documented.

Patterns

Recurring combinations of components that solve specific problems: Search with autocomplete, Empty state with CTA, Data table with sorting.

Guidelines

Written documentation explaining when and how to use each piece. The 'why' behind every decision. Without this, a design system is just a UI kit.

Design Tokens
// Primitive Tokens (never use directly)
color.blue.500 = #0066FF
// Semantic Tokens (use in components)
color.action.primary = color.blue.500
color.action.primary.dark = color.blue.300
// Component Tokens (optional granularity)
button.primary.background = color.action.primary

Stage 5 · Months 10–12

The Professional Designer

Portfolio, collaboration, and career positioning. At this stage, you're not just designing products — you're shaping teams, processes, and design culture.

Portfolio Development

Your portfolio is a UX project itself. It has users (hiring managers), goals (decide to interview you), and flows (case study structure). Apply everything you know.

What Hiring Managers Actually Evaluate
Problem framing: Did you choose an interesting problem? Weak portfolios jump straight to solutions.
Research depth: Did you talk to real users? What did you learn that surprised you? Fabricated research is immediately detectable.
Process transparency: Show your messy middle: rough wireframes, failed concepts, pivot moments.
Decision rationale: Can you explain why you made each design decision? 'It looked clean' is not a reason.
Business impact: Can you tie your design to measurable outcomes? Even estimated impact shows business fluency.
Collaboration story: How did you work with engineers, PMs, stakeholders? Hiring managers want teammates, not lone geniuses.
Case Study Structure
1. Overview — Context, role, team, timeline, and the outcome (lead with impact)
2. Problem — What user/business problem were you solving? Why did it matter?
3. Research — Methods used, key findings, and surprising insights
4. Process — Sketches, wireframes, explorations, and the decisions made along the way
5. Solution — Final design with interactive prototype link
6. Results — Metrics, user quotes, business impact, lessons learned
Golden Rule: 3 deep case studies beat 10 shallow ones. Make every case study answer: "So what?" and "What would you do next?"

Every great case study tells a story. Stories are remembered; bullet points are forgotten.

  1. Establish Stakes — "100,000 users couldn't complete checkout. We were losing $2M/month." Make me care immediately.
  2. Introduce Conflict — "We assumed the problem was the payment form. Research revealed something completely different."
  3. Show the Journey — Your research, explorations, failures, and pivots. Include the human moments.
  4. Reveal the Insight — "The real problem was that users didn't trust the site was secure."
  5. Show the Resolution — Your solution and its rationale. Prototype link. Before/after.
  6. Demonstrate Impact — "Checkout completion increased 34%. Revenue impact: +$680K/month."
Industry Collaboration
Developer Handoff Best Practices
  • Use Figma's Dev Mode — annotate every component with spacing, color variables, and interaction notes
  • Provide redlines for spacing and layout. Specify: margin, padding, gap, column widths, breakpoints
  • Annotate every interactive state: hover, focus, active, loading, error, empty, disabled
  • Link to design system components — engineers should never need to guess about border radius or color values
  • Record a Loom walkthrough of the prototype — 5 minutes of narration prevents days of back-and-forth
Building the Design-Engineering Partnership
  • Invite engineers to design critiques early — they surface feasibility issues before you're attached to solutions
  • Learn basic web principles: the box model, flexbox, responsive breakpoints, CSS variables
  • Understand platform constraints: iOS and Android have different interaction models and native components
  • Never say "make it pixel-perfect" — say "here are the cases that matter most." Pick your battles.
  • Celebrate engineering craftsmanship publicly. Ship wins belong to the whole team.
Design Sprint 0: Stay 1–2 sprints ahead of engineering. Design in sprint N what engineering will build in sprint N+2. Never design and build in the same sprint.
Design in Sprints: Break designs into deliverable increments. Design the happy path first, then edge cases.
Definition of Done: A design is 'done' when: all states are documented, accessibility is verified, dev handoff notes are added, and a prototype walk-through is recorded.
Retros: Use retros to improve design-eng collaboration, not just process. Raise communication issues early.
CEO/C-Suite

Lead with business impact. 'This redesign reduces support tickets by 30%' resonates. 'The spacing is more harmonious' does not.

Product Managers

Frame decisions in terms of user needs and metrics. Show how design choices impact North Star metric. Speak JTBD and AARRR.

Engineers

Be specific and precise. Show constraints you considered. Ask questions, don't prescribe implementation.

Legal/Compliance

Know GDPR, CCPA basics. Lead with risk reduction. 'This consent pattern reduces compliance exposure' wins the room.

The Executive Brief Structure

Problem (1 slide) → Research insight (1–2 slides) → Design exploration (2–3 slides) → Final solution (2–3 slides) → Impact + next steps (1 slide). Total: <10 slides. Every slide title is a complete sentence.

Research Foundations — Portfolio Science & Communication Research
The professional stage is about visibility and influence. These resources are grounded in communication research, hiring psychology, and organizational behavior — the human sciences behind how design careers are actually built.
How Designers Are Evaluated — Hiring Manager Survey Research
NNG / LinkedIn Design Research, multiple years
NNG's annual Design Salary surveys and hiring manager research consistently show: (1) Process transparency is the single most differentiating factor in portfolios. (2) Hiring managers spend 3–5 minutes on a portfolio initially. (3) "I can't see the thinking" is the most common rejection reason. (4) Senior roles require demonstrated business impact, not just craft quality.
Storytelling and Persuasion in Professional Contexts
Heath & Heath — "Made to Stick" (2007)
The SUCCES framework: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories. Applied to design presentations: case studies that follow a narrative arc (problem → tension → insight → solution → impact) are remembered far longer than bullet-point feature lists. The "Curse of Knowledge" principle explains why designers over-explain process instead of focusing on impact.
Psychological Safety & Team Performance Research
Amy Edmondson — Harvard Business School (1999, 2018)
Google's Project Aristotle confirmed Edmondson's research: psychological safety (the belief that you won't be punished for speaking up) is the #1 predictor of team performance — above technical skill, IQ, or experience. For designers, this means creating psychological safety in design reviews, user testing sessions, and stakeholder presentations is not "soft" work — it directly predicts team output quality.
Agile Manifesto & Human-Centered Agile Research
Beck et al. (2001) / Jeff Gothelf & Josh Seiden — "Lean UX" (2013)
Gothelf and Seiden's Lean UX framework is the practical synthesis of Agile development and UX design. Their research showed that traditional "big design up front" approaches are incompatible with Agile sprints, but that "design in a vacuum" creates equally poor outcomes. Their solution: shared understanding over documentation, collaboration over handoffs, and responding to change over following a plan.

Practice Exercises — Stage 5
Exercise 1 — Case Study Rewrite Challenge (4 hrs)
01Find a case study you've done previously — or a project you worked on but never documented.
02Write the case study opening in 3 different ways: (a) problem-first, (b) metric-first ("We increased conversion by 34%…"), (c) user-story-first ("Sara had 90 seconds to…").
03Share all 3 openings with 3 people. Ask which made them want to read more and why.
04Revise using the strongest opening. Apply the 6-step narrative arc to the full case study.
GOALLearn that the same design work can have radically different impact depending on how the story is framed.
Exercise 2 — Mock Design Critique (2 hrs)
01Find a design community (ADPList, Designers.chat, Twitter/X design community) and post 1–2 screens from a personal project for critique.
02When you receive feedback: resist the urge to immediately defend your decisions. First just say "thank you" and take notes.
03After the session, evaluate each piece of feedback against your design rationale. Which feedback was valid? Which was a matter of taste vs. evidence?
04Implement the valid feedback. Write a 1-paragraph "design decision log" explaining what changed and why.
GOALBuild the emotional muscle to receive critique and distinguish signal from noise — essential for professional design environments.
Exercise 3 — Stakeholder Communication Practice (2 hrs)
01Take any design decision you've made (e.g., why you used a bottom tab bar instead of a hamburger menu).
02Write 4 different versions of the rationale — one for a CEO, one for a PM, one for an engineer, one for a marketing director. Each must be under 3 sentences.
03Read each version back and ask: does this speak to what THEY care about, or what I care about?
GOALDesign decisions only survive if they can be communicated to the people who can approve, build, or block them.

Career Practice Exercises
Practice 1 — Weekly Design Review Habit (Ongoing)
01Every Friday, spend 20 minutes on Mobbin, UX Archive, or Pageflows reviewing 5–10 new app screens.
02For each screen, identify: one thing they did brilliantly, one thing you'd change, and one UX principle or law it demonstrates.
03Keep a running "inspiration log" in Notion with screenshots and your annotations.
GOALBuild pattern recognition through consistent exposure. After 6 months, you'll have a personal library of 300+ annotated UI examples.
Practice 2 — Monthly Learning Deep Dive (Ongoing)
01Each month, pick ONE topic from this roadmap that you feel weakest on.
02Spend 4–6 hours: read one article, watch one talk, read one research study, and apply what you learned in one small design exercise.
03Write a 200-word "what I learned" summary in Notion. Teaching it to yourself in writing is how you truly internalize it.
